Data Visualisation 2 - Visualising Formula 1 Driver Data

Fabian Psomas

Formula 1 is the highest category of global motorsport, drivers have been competing for the
World Driver's Championship (WDC) since 1950. F1 is typically thought of as a Euro-centric
competition, but drivers have come from all corners of the globe to participate in the sport.


Where do F1 drivers come from?

As you might expect, most F1 drivers come from Europe, even
after normalising by population. More drivers than you might
expect come from South America. Australian F1 drivers are also
quite common, having the third most drivers per 10 million
people outside of Europe.

F1 Drivers per 10 Million Population

Zooming into Europe, it's clear that western and central European
drivers have been most common in F1, with Switzerland having the most
drivers per 10 million people, followed by the United Kingdom and
Belgium. Swedish and Finnish drivers are also relatively common.
No F1 drivers have come from eastern or southeastern Europe (excluding Russia).

European F1 Drivers per 10 Million Population






Which countries have been most successful in F1?

Going by total championships won, the UK has been the most
successful, followed by Germany and Brazil. This isn't unexpected,
given the absolute and relative large number of drivers that
have come from the UK.

Interestingly, Germany's 12 championships are shared between
only three different drivers; seven-time champion Michael Schumacher,
four-time champion Sebastian Vettel, and one-time winner Nico Rosberg.
Australian drivers have also been relatively successful with
five WDC winners, more than Italy, France or the USA.

F1 Championships by Country

The two most successful drivers of all time, Michael Schumacher
and Lewis Hamilton (both with seven WDC titles each) contribute
greatly to their nation's overall total. The most successful
Australian driver in F1 history is Jack Brabham, who won
three WDC titles in 1959, 1960 and 1966.

Most Successful Drivers

What if we looked at the proportion of unique champions to
total number of drivers from each country, instead of total
championships? From this, we can see that Finnish drivers
tend to be very successful in F1, three of the nine drivers
from Finland that have competed in the sport have become
eventual champions.

Australia is third best according to this metric, behind only
Finland and Austria, with almost 12% of all Australian F1 drivers
becoming champions. Perhaps most interestingly, Australian drivers
tend to be more successful (relatively speaking) than western
European nations like the UK, Italy and France, with only 6%,
2% and 1% of their drivers becoming champions respectively.
